Big startups benefitting the most in the funding boom

The big select startups are benefitting the most in this sustained funding boom. Reports show that nearly half of the $24.5 billion in total funds raised by Indian startups between January and September was invested in 73 companies, in follow-on rounds. Since January, up to $12.9 billion has been invested into startups that had already scooped up previous rounds, indicating how the top two-three startups across high-growth sectors were cornering the majority of investment capital. Investors are now more confident in the market's depth and are doubling down on bets at higher valuations. Risk investors are now eager to put more money into top companies planning IPOs in the next two to three years. This is inspired by the successful public market listing of Zomato. This funding boom and the emergence of top players with massive war chests would result in a lot of mergers and acquisitions in the next two to three years, similar to what is already happening in the ed-tech segment by well-funded companies such as Unacademy and Byju's. A lot of these well-funded startups would end up acquiring companies and adjacencies in their field since purchasing a team, a distributor channel, or an upstream manufacturer would allow them to control the entire stack.

The Inventor

 

The Inventor looks into the rise and fall of Theranos, a multibillion-dollar biotech firm that seemed poised to revolutionise the healthcare business. The focus of the film is on Elizabeth Holmes, the company's founder, and to a lesser extent, Sunny Balwani, the company's COO.

Theranos meteoric rise and catastrophic fall exemplifies the Silicon Valley attitude towards valuation. This should serve as a reminder to all of us to never get carried away by promises that sound too good to be true.
